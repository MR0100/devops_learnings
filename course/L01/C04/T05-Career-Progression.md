# L01/C04/T05 — Career Progression: IC1 → Staff → Principal → Distinguished

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the IC ladder at FAANGM
- Identify the criteria that distinguish each level
- Build a level-up plan

## The Generic FAANGM Ladder

| Level | Common Titles | Scope | Years XP |
|---|---|---|---|
| L3 / E3 / IC1 | Engineer | Owns tasks within a service | 0–2 |
| L4 / E4 / IC2 | Engineer II | Owns a service or component | 2–5 |
| L5 / E5 / IC3 | Senior Engineer | Owns multiple services / leads features | 5–10 |
| L6 / E6 / IC4 | Staff Engineer | Owns subsystem or domain across teams | 10–15 |
| L7 / E7 / IC5 | Senior Staff / Principal | Owns architecture across the org | 12–20 |
| L8 / E8 / IC6 | Sr Principal / Distinguished | Industry influence, multi-org architecture | 15+ |

## What Distinguishes Each Level

### Junior (L3/L4)
- Executes well on defined tasks
- Learns the stack
- Writes correct code, debugs effectively

### Senior (L5)
- Designs features and services
- Owns reliability of their services
- Mentors junior engineers
- Operates with limited oversight

### Staff (L6)
- Owns a domain across multiple teams
- Drives multi-quarter technical strategy
- Influences team behavior without authority
- Handles ambiguity at the org level
- Sets technical direction; gets others to follow

### Principal (L7)
- Owns architecture for the BU or company
- Influences external (partners, OSS, conferences)
- Mentors staff engineers
- Identifies billion-dollar problems before others see them

### Distinguished (L8+)
- Industry-wide influence
- Mentors principal engineers
- Defines the company's strategic technical bets

## The Skill Shift at Each Step

```
L3 ──► L4: deeper craft, broader code ownership
L4 ──► L5: design, mentorship
L5 ──► L6: cross-team influence, ambiguity
L6 ──► L7: multi-domain architecture, org-level impact
L7 ──► L8: industry influence
```

The biggest shift is L5 → L6: from individual contributor to *force multiplier*.

## The "Pillars of Staff Engineer" (Will Larson)

1. Technical Excellence
2. Project Execution
3. Cross-functional Influence
4. Direction Setting
5. Cross-team Communication

## What Doesn't Get You Promoted

- Doing more of what got you to your current level
- Working harder (capacity is finite)
- Volume of code committed
- Number of tickets closed

## What Does Get You Promoted

- **Scope expansion** — visible impact across teams
- **Force multiplication** — your work makes others better
- **Strategic vision** — you see what others don't
- **Track record** — repeated delivery on hard problems
- **Sponsor + Sponsor's sponsor** — people advocating for you

## A 24-Month Plan Template

| Quarter | Theme | Deliverable |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 | Master craft in current scope | Own a critical service end-to-end |
| Q2 | Earn one cross-team project | Lead a 2-team initiative |
| Q3 | Build sponsorship | Identify and partner with a staff+ sponsor |
| Q4 | Demonstrate scope | Pitch and own a multi-quarter initiative |
| Q5 | Deliver the initiative | Tangible impact, write postmortems and design docs |
| Q6 | Document influence | Public artifacts, talks, retros |
| Q7 | Promotion case prep | Self-review, peer review, manager alignment |
| Q8 | Promotion or recalibration | Get promoted or learn why not |

## Interview Prep

**Mid → Senior**: "Tell me about a time you went above your scope."

**Senior → Staff**: "Tell me about a time you influenced a decision without authority."

**Staff → Principal**: "What's a multi-quarter technical bet you made and what was the outcome?"
