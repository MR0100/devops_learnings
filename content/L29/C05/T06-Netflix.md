# L29/C05/T06 — Netflix (Keeper Test, Freedom & Responsibility)

## Learning Objectives

- Prep Netflix's senior-only bar and culture-driven loop
- Demonstrate senior judgment and "stunning colleague" caliber, not just correctness
- Understand the all-cash comp model and the keeper-test trade-off

## Roles

Netflix hires **senior and above only** — there is effectively no junior tier:

- **Senior Software Engineer**
- **Senior Staff / Staff**
- **Principal**

The DevOps/SRE-equivalent work (platform, reliability, core infra) runs on these same senior bands. If you're not operating at a clear senior level, Netflix is not the right application yet.

## Levels — A Flat Ladder

Netflix deliberately keeps a **flat structure** with few levels: Senior → Senior Staff → Principal. There's no title ladder to climb micro-step by micro-step; the expectation is that everyone is already operating at a high, autonomous level. This is the structural expression of the culture: hire fewer, more senior people, pay them top-of-market, and give them freedom.

## Process

1. Recruiter screen
2. Phone screens (often multiple)
3. Onsite (intense, many interviewers):
   - Coding (1-2; practical, medium-hard)
   - System design (1-2; real-world, complex)
   - Behavioral (1-2; **culture-fit heavy**, senior-level only)
   - Hiring manager + senior team members

The loop weights **culture fit and senior judgment** unusually heavily. Technical competence is assumed at this level; the differentiating question is "is this a stunning colleague we'd fight to keep?"

## Culture — Freedom & Responsibility

Netflix's culture is its product, and the loop tests for it directly:

- **Freedom & Responsibility** — minimal process and approvals; you're trusted to make big calls and accountable for them. Show you've operated *without* a safety net of process.
- **Context, not control** — leaders give context and let you decide; you're expected to act on judgment, not wait for instructions.
- **High performance** — the team is a "pro sports team, not a family." Everyone is expected to be excellent.
- **Candor** — direct, frequent feedback is the norm.

In stories, demonstrate that you've thrived with autonomy: made a consequential decision yourself, owned the outcome, and didn't need to be managed closely.

## The Keeper Test

The defining cultural mechanism: managers continually ask, **"Would I fight to keep this person if they were leaving?"** If the honest answer is no, the person is given a **generous severance** and let go. There are no performance-improvement plans in the traditional sense.

What it means for you:

- The bar is **high and continuous** — they're hiring someone they'd fight to keep, not someone who's merely adequate.
- It's a **high-bar, high-pay, lower-job-security** trade. Excellent autonomous performers thrive; people who need structure or coasting room don't.
- In the interview, every signal of "stunning colleague" — judgment, ownership, raising the team's level — directly answers the keeper-test question they're implicitly scoring.

## Coding

- **Medium-to-hard**, but **practical** rather than puzzle-y — Netflix favors problems that resemble real engineering over contest tricks.
- Clean, correct, well-reasoned code with good judgment about trade-offs.

## System Design

- Senior+, complex, and grounded in real-world scale (Netflix streams to hundreds of millions; their infra and chaos-engineering heritage are legendary).
- Expect depth on reliability, scale, and operational resilience — areas where a strong DevOps/SRE candidate shines.

## Behavioral — Senior Stories Only

The behavioral round is explicitly calibrated to **senior judgment**. "Tell me a story about a hard decision" must showcase senior-level scope: a consequential call you owned, a trade-off you navigated with incomplete information, a time you raised the bar for a team. **Mid-level stories sink you** here — a tale of executing a well-defined task, however well, signals you're below the bar. Pick your biggest, most autonomous, highest-judgment stories.

## Sample Q&A

**Q: "Tell me about the most consequential decision you've made."** Netflix wants senior scope and autonomy. Show a high-stakes call you made (often without full information or approval), why, and how you owned the outcome — good or bad. The autonomy is the signal.

**Q: "Tell me about a time you gave or received hard feedback."** Candor is core. Show you can be direct and professional, and that you act on feedback without ego — the "candor" the culture runs on.

**Q: "Why Netflix?"** Reference the culture honestly — freedom, responsibility, high performance — and the technical scale. The wrong answer treats it as just another FAANG; the culture is the whole point and they screen for people who actively want it.

## Specific to Netflix

- Bring **only senior-level stories** with autonomy and consequential judgment.
- Show you thrive with **freedom and minimal process** — you don't need to be managed.
- Be honest about wanting the high-bar / high-pay / lower-security trade; feigned enthusiasm reads false.
- Lean on reliability and scale depth in design — Netflix respects operational rigor.

## Compensation

- **All-cash, top-of-market.** Netflix's model is famous: rather than a base + RSU mix, they pay a high salary and let *you* decide how much (if any) to allocate to stock options. There's no four-year golden-handcuff vesting schedule.
- Often **higher total cash than FAANG** at the same level, with full liquidity — no waiting on vests.
- The trade-off: no large equity upside if the stock soars, and the keeper-test means less job security. You're paid for the risk in cash, up front.

This changes negotiation: there's no RSU/sign-on dance — it's a salary number, and you compare it against the *risk-adjusted* total comp of an equity-heavy offer. (See [C06](../C06/README.md).)

## Best Practices

- Apply only if you're genuinely operating at senior+; the bar is real.
- Prepare your highest-scope, most-autonomous stories.
- Demonstrate thriving under freedom and accountability.
- Lean into reliability/scale depth for design rounds.
- Compare the all-cash offer on a risk-adjusted basis, not headline-to-headline.

## Common Mistakes

- Mid-level stories about executing well-defined tasks — an instant level miss.
- Applying when not yet at the senior bar.
- Treating Netflix like any other FAANG and ignoring the culture screen.
- Underrating the keeper-test / job-security trade-off when accepting.
- Comparing the all-cash number to an equity offer's headline without risk-adjusting.

## Interview Prep

**Junior**: "Should I apply to Netflix?" — Only if I'm genuinely operating at a senior level — Netflix has no junior tier and a flat, senior-only ladder. The bar is 'someone they'd fight to keep,' so a mid-level profile won't clear it; I'd build senior scope elsewhere first.

**Mid**: "What kind of stories does Netflix want?" — Senior-scope, autonomous ones: a consequential decision I owned, a trade-off I navigated with incomplete information, a time I raised a team's bar. A story about executing a well-defined task — however well — reads as below-bar and sinks me, so I lead with my highest-judgment work.

**Senior**: "What is the keeper test and how does it shape the interview?" — Managers continually ask 'would I fight to keep this person?'; if not, you get generous severance, not a PIP. So they're hiring a stunning colleague, not someone adequate, and I make every signal of judgment, ownership, and team-level impact answer that implicit question.

**Staff**: "How do you evaluate Netflix's all-cash offer?" — There's no RSU or sign-on dance — it's a high salary, fully liquid, with optional stock allocation. I compare it risk-adjusted against an equity-heavy offer: Netflix trades equity upside and some job security for guaranteed top-of-market cash now. If I value liquidity and autonomy over lottery-ticket upside, it's compelling; I price in the keeper-test security trade explicitly.
