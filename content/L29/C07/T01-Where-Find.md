# L29/C07/T01 — Where to Find Mock Interviews

## Learning Objectives

- Get practice interviews
- Use feedback

## Sources

### Pramp
Free; peer-to-peer.

### interviewing.io
Anonymous; senior engineers.
Free + paid tiers.

### Codility
Self-paced + coaching.

### Friends
Engineering friends.
Cheap; biased sometimes.

### Coaches
Paid; high quality.
- Career Hub
- Pathrise
- Independent

### Bootcamps
Some include mocks.

### Yourself
Whiteboard alone; record + review.

## Frequency

For onsite prep:
- 10-20 mocks total
- Weekly initially
- Daily near interview

## Per Round Type

- Coding: most
- System design: 5-10
- Behavioral: 3-5

## Get Feedback

- What went well
- What to improve
- Specific actions

## Iterate

- Mock
- Feedback
- Practice
- Mock again

## Time

Pre-interview prep:
- 1-2 months
- Mock weekly
- Real interviews start: rotate

## Tools

- Excalidraw (whiteboarding)
- CoderPad (coding)
- Zoom / Meet (video)

## Best Practices

- Treat like real
- Time-bound
- Talk through
- Take notes after

## Common Mistakes

- Skip mocks
- Friends only (soft)
- No feedback
- Don't iterate

## Interview Prep

**Junior**: "Where do you get mock interviews?" — Pramp for free peer-to-peer practice and interviewing.io for anonymous mocks with real senior engineers. I treat them like the real thing — timed, talking out loud — because solo whiteboarding doesn't train the narration muscle that the real loop scores.

**Mid**: "How many mocks, and of what kind?" — Roughly 10-20 total before an onsite, weighted toward coding with 5-10 system-design and 3-5 behavioral mocks. I ramp from weekly to near-daily as the interview approaches, and I make sure design and behavioral get reps, since those are the rounds people under-practice.

**Senior**: "Why not just practice with friends?" — Friends are convenient but soft — they hesitate to give hard feedback and often aren't calibrated to the FAANGM bar. I use them for early reps, then move to interviewing.io or a paid coach for honest, calibrated signal, because the feedback is the entire point of a mock.

**Staff**: "How do you get the most out of each mock?" — I run it under real constraints — plain editor, strict timer, full narration — then extract specific, actionable feedback ('you skipped scoping,' 'no complexity stated') and turn each into a targeted drill before the next mock. The loop is mock → feedback → targeted practice → re-mock, not just accumulating reps.
