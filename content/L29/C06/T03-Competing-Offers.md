# L29/C06/T03 — Competing Offers

## Learning Objectives

- Use competing offers as the single strongest negotiation lever — honestly
- Sequence your interview timing so offers land close together
- Push back on exploding deadlines without losing the offer

## Why Competing Offers Are the Top Lever

A recruiter can talk all day about how much they value you, but a **competing offer is the only signal a company can't argue with**. It converts your negotiation from "please give me more" into "the market has priced me at X — can you match or beat?" Recruiters expect it, and a real competing offer is what unlocks their authority to go back to the hiring manager for a bump. Everything else in negotiation is secondary to having genuine alternatives.

## Getting Multiple Offers — Timing Is Everything

The hard part isn't asking — it's having two live offers at once:

- **Apply broadly and in parallel**, not one company at a time.
- **Sequence the processes** so they finish close together. If one company is ahead, you can sometimes slow it (ask for a few more days) or speed up the others (tell their recruiter you have a timeline).
- **Aim for 2-3 offers within the same 1-2 week window** — that's when leverage is maximal, because each can be played against the others.

The most common failure is a single offer with an exploding deadline and nothing to compare it to.

## Disclosing an Offer — How to Phrase It

Be direct but collaborative:

```
"I'm really excited about this role. I do have another offer at $X total comp.
Your offer is my preference — can you match or improve it so I can sign?"
```

Or, when you want to keep the company name vague:

```
"I'm weighing an offer at $X from another top company. What can we do to close the gap?"
```

Signaling that *they're your preference* gives the recruiter a reason to fight for you — you're handing them a winnable deal, not a threat.

## Never Lie

Do not fabricate an offer or inflate a real one:

- Companies sometimes **verify** (ask for the offer letter, or cross-check via shared recruiting networks).
- A lie caught means the **offer is rescinded** and your reputation in a small industry takes a hit.
- You can decline to share specifics, but **never invent numbers**. "I'd rather not name the company, but the total comp is $X" is fine; a made-up $X is not.

## What to Share, and What to Hold Back

| Disclose | Hold / use judgment |
|---|---|
| Total comp of the competing offer | The company name (often optional) |
| That you have a real alternative | Exact component breakdown |
| Your timeline | Your *current* salary (never reveal) |

Share total comp because that's the number they'll match against; protect the rest as leverage.

## What Actually Improves With a Competing Offer

Same ranking as any negotiation, but a competing offer unlocks more movement:

- **Sign-on** — most flexible, moves first and most.
- **RSU grant** — a competing offer is exactly what frees recruiters to raise this.
- **Base** — band-capped; small movement.
- **Level** — rarely, but a competing offer *at the higher level* is the strongest level argument ([T02](T02-Level-Negotiation.md)).

## Handling Exploding Deadlines

Recruiters create urgency — "we need an answer by Friday" — to prevent you from gathering competing offers. Push back politely; deadlines are almost always softer than stated:

```
"I'm very interested and want to make the right long-term decision.
I have another process wrapping up next week — could we extend to [date]?"
```

This is standard and reasonable; a company that refuses any extension is showing you something about how they operate.

## Walking Power

Your leverage is directly proportional to your willingness to walk:

- **If you can walk** (you have another good offer or your current job is fine), you negotiate from strength and get better counters.
- **If you must take this one**, you're weaker — so build alternatives *before* you need them, and avoid signaling desperation.

## Best Practices

- Run processes in parallel and sequence them to finish close together.
- Lead with "you're my preference" when disclosing a competing offer.
- Share total comp; protect the rest as leverage; never reveal current salary.
- Push back on exploding deadlines — they're usually negotiable.
- Negotiate from genuine walking power.

## Common Mistakes

- A single offer with no alternative and no leverage.
- Lying about or inflating a competing offer — caught means rescinded.
- Accepting an exploding deadline instead of asking for more time.
- Revealing current salary, anchoring the new offer low.
- Negotiating from desperation with no willingness to walk.

## Interview Prep

**Junior**: "Why is a competing offer so powerful?" — It's the one signal a company can't argue with. It turns the conversation from 'please pay me more' into 'the market priced me at X — can you match?' and it unlocks the recruiter's authority to go back to the hiring manager for a bump.

**Mid**: "How do you phrase disclosing it?" — Directly and collaboratively: 'I'm excited and you're my preference — I have another offer at $X total comp, can you match or improve it so I can sign?' Framing them as my first choice gives the recruiter a winnable deal to fight for instead of a threat.

**Senior**: "How do you get two offers at the same time?" — By running processes in parallel and sequencing them to finish in the same one-to-two-week window — slowing the company that's ahead with a few extra days, speeding the others by citing a timeline. A single offer with an exploding deadline and nothing to compare is the weak position I work to avoid.

**Staff**: "A recruiter gives you a Friday deadline and you're mid-process elsewhere." — I push back politely — exploding deadlines are almost always softer than stated. I say I'm very interested, want to make the right long-term call, and have another process wrapping next week, and ask to extend. That's standard; if they refuse any extension at all, that itself tells me something about how the company operates, and I never bluff or inflate the competing number since companies do verify.
