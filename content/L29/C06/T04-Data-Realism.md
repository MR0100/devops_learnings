# L29/C06/T04 — levels.fyi & TeamBlind Realism

## Learning Objectives

- Use crowd-sourced comp data to set a realistic, defensible ask
- Read the data's biases so you don't over- or under-shoot
- Adjust for level, location, recency, and stock movement before quoting a number

## Why Data-Backed Negotiation Wins

A negotiation ask grounded in data reads as informed and reasonable; a number pulled from thin air reads as greedy or naive. "Based on levels.fyi, the 75th percentile for this level and location is $X, and my signal was strong" is a sentence a recruiter can take to a hiring manager. "I want $X" is not. The two essential tools are **levels.fyi** for the numbers and **TeamBlind** for the context and tactics.

## levels.fyi

Crowd-sourced compensation data, broken down by:

- **Company** and **level** (with the cross-company level mapping that's invaluable for comparing offers)
- **Location** (Bay Area vs Seattle vs remote vs international)
- **Component breakdown** — base / stock / bonus, not just total
- **Recency** — filter to recent data points; old grants reflect old stock prices

Use it to find the **median and 75th percentile** for your exact level and location, and to translate one company's level into another's (see [C01/T05](../C01/T05-Levels.md)).

## TeamBlind

Anonymous professional forum where engineers share what levels.fyi can't:

- **Negotiation war stories** — what bumps people actually got and how.
- **Real, recent numbers** — sometimes more current than levels.fyi.
- **Company culture and team intel** — on-call load, reorg risk, manager reputation.

Treat it as **directional, not gospel** — it skews toward extremes (big wins and bitter complaints) and toward higher numbers than typical. Useful for tactics and sentiment; calibrate the numbers against levels.fyi.

## How to Use the Data Before Negotiating

1. **Pin your exact level and location** — comp swings enormously across both.
2. **Pull median and 75th percentile** total comp for that cell.
3. **Filter to recent data points** — within the last year ideally.
4. **Break it into components** — know whether the gap to your offer is base, stock, or sign-on, so you target the right lever.
5. **Set your ask** — median if your signal was average, 75th-90th percentile if it was strong and you have a competing offer.

## Reading the Biases

Crowd-sourced data has known skews — account for them:

- **Self-selection:** people report big offers more than mediocre ones → data skews **high**.
- **Stale grants:** an RSU grant's *reported* value used the stock price at grant time; today's value differs. Re-price at current valuation.
- **Location-specific:** a Bay Area number doesn't apply to Austin or London.
- **Survivorship on Blind:** loudest voices are the outliers.

## Adjusting for the Real World

- **Stock movement:** RSU comp is only as good as the stock. Negotiate against the *current* share price, and remember a 4-year-old data point's TC may be worth more or less today.
- **Geography:** SF Bay and NYC are highest; Seattle high; Austin/Denver mid-high; many remote roles carry a location discount. Internationally, London ≈ 60-70% of SF, with Tel Aviv high and PPP-adjusted markets (e.g. India) lower in absolute terms.
- **Market cycle:** comp inflated sharply in 2021-22, then cooled; bands and refresher generosity vary by company and year. Don't quote a peak-2022 number in a softer market.

## Setting a Realistic Ask

Do:
- Ask for the **75th-90th percentile** when your signal is strong and backed by a competing offer.
- **Cite the data** explicitly in the conversation.
- Make a **reasonable counter** the recruiter can actually act on.

Don't:
- Demand top-of-band with no justification or data.
- Quote stale or wrong-location numbers.
- Reflexively refuse a fair offer chasing an outlier you saw on Blind.

## Best Practices

- Research level + location specifically before any negotiation conversation.
- Use recent data points; re-price stock at the current valuation.
- Combine levels.fyi numbers with TeamBlind tactics, calibrating Blind's skew.
- Anchor your ask to median (average signal) or 75th-90th percentile (strong signal + competing offer).
- Adjust for geography and the current market cycle.

## Common Mistakes

- Negotiating with no data at all.
- Quoting stale grants or peak-cycle numbers in a softer market.
- Taking TeamBlind outliers as the norm and demanding unrealistic comp.
- Ignoring location and re-pricing, so the target is simply wrong.
- Settling low because you never looked up the band.

## Quick Refs

```
levels.fyi:  bands by company/level/location/recency + level mapping
TeamBlind:   tactics, sentiment, recent numbers — directional, skews high
Ask:         median (avg signal) | 75-90th %ile (strong + competing offer)
Adjust:      recent data, current stock price, location, market cycle
Bias:        self-selection skews high; re-price stale RSU grants
```

## Interview Prep

**Junior**: "Why negotiate with data?" — Because a data-backed ask reads as informed and reasonable, while a number from nowhere reads as greedy. 'levels.fyi puts the 75th percentile for this level and location at $X' is something a recruiter can take to the hiring manager; 'I want $X' is not.

**Mid**: "How do you use levels.fyi and TeamBlind together?" — levels.fyi for the numbers — median and 75th percentile filtered to my exact level, location, and recent data — and TeamBlind for tactics, sentiment, and team intel. I treat Blind as directional since it skews toward big wins and outliers, and I calibrate its numbers against levels.fyi.

**Senior**: "What biases do you correct for in the data?" — Self-selection skews it high since people report big offers more; reported RSU values use the grant-time stock price, so I re-price at the current valuation; and numbers are location- and cycle-specific. I won't quote a Bay Area peak-2022 figure for an Austin role in a softer market.

**Staff**: "How do you set the actual ask?" — I pin my exact level and location, pull median and 75th percentile recent total comp, and break it into components so I know whether the gap is base, stock, or sign-on — then I target the right lever. With a strong signal and a competing offer I ask for the 75th-90th percentile; with an average signal I anchor near median and make a counter the recruiter can realistically act on.
