# L29/C01/T04 — Bar Raiser / Hiring Committee

## Learning Objectives

- Understand how the hire/no-hire decision is actually made — and who holds the power at each company
- Recognize the bar raiser round and what it specifically tests
- Build a consistent, level-appropriate narrative that survives an independent calibrator

## Why a Separate Decision Body Exists

Left to themselves, hiring managers over-hire: they're under pressure to fill a seat and they like the candidate in front of them. To counter that, FAANGM companies separate the *decision* from the *interviewers*. The mechanism differs by company, but the goal is the same — a consistent bar and reduced individual bias. Knowing which mechanism you're facing changes where you focus.

## Amazon — The Bar Raiser

The bar raiser is a trained interviewer from **outside the hiring team** who sits in the loop specifically to protect the company-wide hiring bar. Key facts:

- **Veto power.** The bar raiser can block a hire even if the hiring manager and everyone else says yes. This is the only role with that power.
- **Independent and incentive-free.** They don't report to the team and gain nothing from filling the seat, so they optimize purely for "is this person above the bar for this level?"
- **Behavioral-heavy and strict.** Expect tough Leadership Principle questions and hard follow-ups ("dive deep" on your specifics). They probe for the *raise-the-bar* signal: is this candidate better than 50% of current Amazonians at this level?

Prep implication: bring extra, well-structured LP stories and be ready for relentless depth follow-ups. (See [C04/T02](../C04/T02-Amazon-LPs.md) and [C05/T01](../C05/T01-Amazon.md).)

## Google — The Hiring Committee

Google removes the decision from the interviewers entirely. After the onsite, your written feedback is assembled into a **packet** and sent to an independent **hiring committee (HC)** of senior engineers who *did not interview you*.

- **No interviewer veto.** No single interviewer — not even the hiring manager — can hire or reject you alone. The HC decides on the packet.
- **They read, they don't meet you.** This makes the *written* quality of your interviewers' notes decisive, which means clarity and memorable specifics in your answers matter — vague answers produce vague notes.
- **Bias reduction is the point.** The committee calibrates across many candidates, so the bar is consistent rather than dependent on who happened to interview you.

## The Packet and the Decision

The artifact the decision body weighs typically contains:

- Every interviewer's written feedback and vote
- A **leveling assessment** (are you at L5? L6?)
- The hiring manager's support (or lack of it)
- The bar raiser's opinion (Amazon) or the committee's calibration (Google)

Possible outcomes are not just hire/no-hire:

| Outcome | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Hire | Above the bar at the target level |
| No hire | Below the bar |
| **Down-level** | Strong, but at the level below — offer at the lower level |
| Up-level | Rare; signal exceeded the target level |

A **down-level** is common and worth thinking about in advance: accept (foot in the door, promote later) or decline (try elsewhere or re-apply at level). Decide your floor *before* the offer call so you're not negotiating against your own ego in the moment.

## Who Holds the Power, by Company

The single most useful thing to internalize — it tells you where to invest:

| Company | Decision driver |
|---|---|
| Amazon | Bar raiser (external, veto) |
| Google | Hiring committee (independent, packet-based) |
| Meta | Hiring manager (strong) + committee for leveling |
| Apple | Hiring team / manager (team-assembled loop) |
| Microsoft | Hiring manager (no external bar raiser) |
| Netflix | Hiring manager + senior team (keeper-test bar) |

Where the manager drives (Meta, Apple, Microsoft), invest in manager rapport. Where an external body drives (Amazon, Google), invest in a consistent, well-articulated narrative that survives someone who never met you.

## Timeline

- **Debrief:** 1-3 days after the onsite, interviewers meet (or the packet is finalized).
- **Committee:** at Google, HC review can add 1-2 weeks.
- **Offer:** follows the decision, then comp negotiation begins ([C06](../C06/README.md)).

Silence in this window is normal and not a signal; the process is just slow.

## Best Practices

- Be strong and *consistent* across all rounds — the decision is made on the aggregate.
- Tell memorable, specific stories so interviewer notes are vivid, not vague (critical for Google's packet).
- Bring extra behavioral depth for the bar raiser; expect relentless follow-ups.
- Know your target level and your down-level floor before the offer call.
- Invest rapport where the manager drives the decision; invest narrative consistency where an external body does.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating the bar raiser as just another round and under-preparing behavioral depth.
- Inconsistent leveling signal — strong design but junior-sounding stories.
- Giving vague answers that produce vague written feedback (fatal in a packet-based process).
- Not deciding in advance how to handle a down-level offer.
- Reading post-onsite silence as rejection and disengaging.

## Quick Refs

```
Amazon:  bar raiser — external, behavioral-heavy, VETO power
Google:  hiring committee — packet-based, no interviewer veto
Meta/Apple/MSFT: hiring manager drives → invest in rapport
Packet:  feedback + level assessment + HM support + bar raiser
Outcomes: hire / no hire / down-level / (rare) up-level
Timeline: debrief 1-3 days; HC adds 1-2 weeks; silence ≠ rejection
```

## Interview Prep

**Junior**: "What's a bar raiser?" — At Amazon, a trained interviewer from outside the hiring team who protects the company-wide bar and has veto power over the hire. They lean behavioral and strict, so I bring extra, well-structured Leadership Principle stories and expect deep follow-ups on the specifics.

**Mid**: "How is the final decision made at Google?" — The interviewers don't decide; my written feedback becomes a packet that an independent hiring committee — people who never met me — reviews. That makes vivid, specific answers important, because vague answers produce vague notes, and the committee only sees the notes.

**Senior**: "Where should you focus given who holds the power?" — It depends on the company. Where the manager drives — Meta, Apple, Microsoft — I invest in genuine rapport and team fit. Where an external body drives — Amazon's bar raiser, Google's committee — I focus on a consistent, clearly-articulated narrative that survives someone who never interviewed me.

**Staff**: "They offer you a down-level. How do you handle it?" — I decide my floor before the call so I'm not negotiating against my own ego. A down-level means strong-but-below-target; I weigh foot-in-the-door-and-promote against re-applying at level elsewhere, and I ask the recruiter what the leveling assessment actually flagged so I know whether it's a scope gap I can close or a hard bar I won't move.
