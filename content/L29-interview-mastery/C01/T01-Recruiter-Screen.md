# L29/C01/T01 — Recruiter Screen

## Learning Objectives

- Pass the recruiter screen — the gate that decides whether you enter the loop at all
- Set your level and comp expectations without anchoring yourself low
- Turn the recruiter into an ally who advocates for you through the process

## Where It Sits in the Funnel

The recruiter screen is the first conversation, usually 15-30 minutes, and it is **non-technical** — the recruiter is not an engineer and will not assess your code. Their job is to confirm you're real, roughly the right level, interested for genuine reasons, and inside the comp band before the company spends engineer-hours on you. It is a low bar, but a surprising number of strong engineers fumble it by being vague about interest, anchoring their comp too low, or treating the recruiter as an obstacle rather than a partner.

The mental reframe that matters: **the recruiter is on your side once you're in the loop.** They are measured on closes. A recruiter who likes you and believes you'll accept will fight for your level and your offer internally. Win them early.

## The Four Things They're Checking

A recruiter screen almost always covers the same four areas:

| Area | What they ask | What they're confirming |
|---|---|---|
| Background | "Walk me through your background." | You're real and roughly the level the req wants |
| Interest | "Why us?" / "Why now?" | You're not mass-applying; you'll actually accept |
| Compensation | "What are your expectations?" | You're inside the band before engineers invest time |
| Logistics | Timeline, format, location | You're available and the process can move |

## The Resume Walk

You'll be asked some version of "tell me about your background." Have a **60-90 second** pitch rehearsed cold:

- **Current role + scope** — one sentence on what you own and at what scale.
- **One or two highlights** — a migration, an incident you led, a system you built, with a number attached.
- **What you want next** — the kind of work/scope you're targeting, framed to match their role.

Keep it tight. Rambling for four minutes here is the most common own-goal; the recruiter is listening for a clear, confident narrative, not your whole résumé read aloud.

## "Why This Company"

Show specific, researched interest — mission, a product, the scale of their infra, a recent launch, the team's domain. The fatal answer is "I'll take any FAANGM," which signals you'll bail the moment a competing offer lands. Two sentences of genuine specificity ("you run one of the largest Kubernetes fleets in the world and I want to operate at that scale") beats a paragraph of generic praise.

## Compensation Expectations — Handle With Care

The recruiter will ask what you're looking for. This is the highest-stakes moment of the call, because a number stated here can anchor your entire offer. Three good plays:

- **Defer to learn the band:** "I'd like to understand the band for this level before naming a number — what range does this role target?" Often the recruiter just tells you, and now *they've* anchored.
- **Give a researched range:** "Looking at total comp, I'm targeting roughly $X-Y based on levels.fyi data for this level and location." A range backed by data reads as informed, not greedy.
- **Redirect to total comp:** never volunteer your *current* salary. In many US states it's illegal for them to ask, and revealing it caps your offer at a small bump over today.

Do **not** low-ball yourself to seem agreeable, and do not give a single precise number too early. (Full negotiation mechanics are in [C06](../C06/README.md).)

## Logistics — and Reading Red Flags

Confirm the timeline, interview format (virtual vs onsite), the number and type of rounds, and reschedule policy. A good recruiter volunteers all of this and tells you exactly what each round assesses — sometimes even the rubric. Treat that as a gift and prepare accordingly.

Watch for red flags that a process (or company) is disorganized: a vague or shifting timeline, pressure to commit on comp before you've even interviewed, or refusal to share the interview format. One of these is normal noise; several together are a signal to lower your expectations of the org.

## Your Questions

Always have 2-3 ready — having none reads as low interest:

- "What's this team's focus and what would I own?"
- "What's the level and comp range for the role?"
- "What does the interview loop look like, and what does each round assess?"
- "What's the timeline you're working toward?"

## Best Practices

- Research the company and team before the call; reference something specific.
- Rehearse the 60-90 second pitch until it's automatic.
- Give comp as a data-backed range, or defer to learn the band first.
- Never reveal current salary; redirect to target total comp.
- Treat the recruiter as an ally and follow up promptly with anything they ask for.

## Common Mistakes

- Showing up underprepared and rambling through the resume walk.
- Revealing current compensation, anchoring the offer low.
- Generic "any FAANGM" interest with no specific reason.
- Asking zero questions, signaling you don't care.
- Naming a single precise comp number before you understand the band.

## Quick Refs

```
Length:  15-30 min, non-technical
Covers:  background | interest | comp | logistics
Pitch:   60-90 sec — scope + 1-2 highlights + what's next
Comp:    range backed by data, or defer to learn the band; never reveal current
Ally:    recruiter is measured on closes — win them early
Always:  2-3 researched questions ready
```

## Interview Prep

**Junior**: "Give me your background." — I deliver a rehearsed 60-90 second pitch: my current role and scale in one line, one or two highlights with a number attached, and what I want next framed to their role — then stop, rather than reading my whole résumé.

**Mid**: "What are your comp expectations?" — I give a data-backed range from levels.fyi for the level and location, or ask the recruiter for the band first so they anchor. I never reveal my current salary, since that just caps the offer at a small bump.

**Senior**: "Why this company?" — I name something specific and true — their infra scale, a product, the team's domain — because a generic 'any FAANGM' answer signals I'll bail on a competing offer, and recruiters screen hard for genuine intent at senior level.

**Staff**: "How do you use the recruiter relationship?" — I treat the recruiter as my internal advocate: they're measured on closes, so I'm responsive, easy to schedule, and clear about what would make me accept. A recruiter who believes I'll sign will push for my level and my number in the debrief, which matters more at Staff where leveling is contested.
